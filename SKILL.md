# SaaS Motion Taste OS

## Purpose

Use this skill as a direction system for premium SaaS motion graphics. Optimize for taste: clear product truth, elegant restraint, precise choreography, crisp hierarchy, motivated transitions, and rhythm that feels intentionally composed.

This skill is tool-agnostic. Do not assume a production package, animation framework, or implementation stack unless the user asks for one.

## Operating Mode

Start by identifying the requested output:

- **Concept direction**: Define the film idea, narrative promise, visual system, and pacing.
- **Storyboard or shot plan**: Produce timed shots with purpose, composition, motion, transition, and copy.
- **Motion critique**: Diagnose taste failures and prescribe fixes.
- **Reference teardown**: Convert a video or demo list into reusable principles and shot patterns.
- **Polish pass**: Improve hierarchy, rhythm, transitions, typography, UI treatment, and finish.

For any substantive video design task, follow this order:

1. Establish the product truth: audience, promise, feature set, emotional tone, and conversion goal.
2. Select the strongest product insights, claims, and proof points from `references/product-insight-selection.md`.
3. Choose the narrative shape from `references/story-structures.md`.
4. Build a shot sequence using `references/shot-archetypes.md`.
5. Define staging and camera behavior from `references/camera-staging.md`.
6. Define choreography from `references/choreography-patterns.md`.
7. Apply product UI and type treatment from `references/product-ui-treatment.md` and `references/typography-layout.md`.
8. Set pacing from `references/timing-rhythm.md`.
9. Format the deliverable using `references/output-contracts.md`.
10. Run the final concept through `references/critique-rubric.md` and `references/anti-patterns.md`.

## Direction Standards

Always make motion decisions in terms of viewer attention:

- Name the focal element in each beat.
- State why it moves.
- State what the viewer should understand after the movement resolves.
- Keep one dominant idea per shot unless the shot is explicitly a crescendo.
- Prefer motivated continuity over decorative motion.
- Prefer specific product meaning over generic "futuristic" visuals.

When writing a shot plan, include:

- Time range or duration.
- Narrative purpose.
- On-screen composition.
- Camera behavior and staging.
- Primary motion.
- Secondary motion.
- Transition in and out.
- Copy or voiceover, if relevant.
- Taste notes: why the shot feels premium and what to avoid.

When critiquing, lead with the highest-impact issues:

- Product truth and narrative clarity.
- Visual hierarchy.
- Motion motivation.
- Timing and pacing.
- Typography and UI polish.
- Finish, texture, depth, and brand consistency.

## Output Constraints

Avoid vague direction such as "make it sleek", "add smooth animations", or "use modern transitions" unless immediately translated into concrete choices. Specify camera behavior, object relationships, timing, hierarchy, easing, negative space, and transition mechanics.

Do not overfill the video. Premium SaaS motion often feels expensive because it removes weak ideas, gives strong ideas room to breathe, and resolves every movement cleanly.

Do not copy any reference video. Extract principles, pacing logic, composition habits, and transition grammar, then adapt them to the user's product truth.
