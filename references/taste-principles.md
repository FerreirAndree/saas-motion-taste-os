# Taste Principles

## Premium SaaS Motion Defined

Premium SaaS motion is not "more animation." It is a controlled sequence of attention decisions that makes a product feel inevitable, useful, intelligent, and well made.

The work should feel:

- Specific: every visual comes from the product, user, workflow, data, or brand idea.
- Composed: the frame has a clear hierarchy before, during, and after motion.
- Motivated: motion explains causality, state change, or product value.
- Restrained: one strong idea is preferred over many competing ideas.
- Tactile: UI, type, cards, charts, and abstract forms have weight, depth, and optical care.
- Rhythmic: beats have anticipation, action, settle, and breathing room.
- Legible: the viewer can understand the promise without pausing.

## The Taste Stack

Evaluate every idea through this stack:

1. **Truth**: What real product capability, user pain, or market shift is this beat expressing?
2. **Point of view**: What does the brand believe that competitors would not say the same way?
3. **Hierarchy**: What must the viewer look at first, second, and third?
4. **Choreography**: How does attention move through the frame?
5. **Continuity**: Why does this shot become the next shot?
6. **Finish**: Are spacing, type, easing, shadows, scale, color, and texture precise?

If a shot fails high in the stack, do not polish it. Replace the idea.

## Premium Signals

Use these signals to make the work feel high-end:

- Fewer simultaneous motions, each with a clear role.
- Confident negative space around the hero object.
- UI details that imply real software, not fake rectangles.
- Subtle parallax and depth that support the idea rather than decorate it.
- Typography that behaves like part of the system, not captions pasted on top.
- Transitions caused by object logic: a card expands into a dashboard, a line becomes a timeline, a cursor action unfolds a workflow.
- Micro-pauses after important reveals.
- Clean endpoint states where every object has resolved.
- Brand color used as a signal, not a wash over everything.
- Soft imperfection only where it adds tactility: grain, shadow, specular edge, glass thickness, screen glow.

## Direction Questions

Before accepting a sequence, answer:

- What is the product promise in one sentence?
- Why is motion the right way to explain it?
- What is the viewer supposed to remember?
- Which beat is the hero reveal?
- Which beat proves usefulness?
- Which beat creates emotional desire?
- Which beat creates trust?
- Which details make this specific to the product?

## Principle: One Beat, One Change

A beat should usually express one clear transformation:

- scattered to organized
- manual to automated
- hidden to visible
- slow to instant
- fragmented to unified
- uncertain to confident
- local action to system-wide impact

Do not combine too many transformations in the same shot. Layer complexity across beats instead.

## Principle: Motion Transfers Attention

Motion is not decoration. It is how the viewer's eye is handed from one idea to the next.

Strong attention transfers include:

- A moving object lands where the next focal element appears.
- A line trace becomes an axis, timeline, connector, or underline.
- A UI selection causes the next panel to open.
- A zoom reveals context that redefines the previous shot.
- Type exits in the direction of the next object.
- A pulse or highlight marks the exact feature the next shot expands.

Weak attention transfers include:

- Random fades.
- Cards sliding in from arbitrary directions.
- Camera moves that reveal nothing new.
- Objects moving because the frame feels empty.

## Principle: Product First, Abstraction Second

Abstraction should clarify the product, not hide it. Use abstract geometry, data streams, particles, or symbolic systems only when they help explain:

- scale
- automation
- intelligence
- connection
- security
- speed
- transformation
- trust

Return to concrete product evidence often enough that the viewer believes the promise.

## Principle: Expensive Restraint

Expensive motion leaves room for the viewer to understand and admire the idea. Use restraint by:

- cutting weak beats
- reducing copy density
- giving hero states a clean hold
- using only one accent color per idea
- avoiding constant camera movement
- making secondary motion quieter than primary motion
- resolving objects fully before the next major beat

## Principle: Specificity Beats Aesthetic

Generic SaaS videos use the same cards, gradients, blobs, and charts for every product. Premium SaaS videos express the exact business logic of the product.

Replace generic visuals with product-specific metaphors:

- "AI assistant" becomes a visible decision path, suggestion confidence, or workflow completion.
- "Analytics" becomes a messy data condition resolving into a specific insight.
- "Collaboration" becomes ownership, handoff, approval, or shared context.
- "Automation" becomes an action chain where each step visibly causes the next.
- "Security" becomes permission boundaries, audit trails, isolation, verification, or calm control.

## Principle: Finish Is Behavior

Polish is not only visual styling. It is how elements behave:

- Does the card stop with conviction?
- Does the type snap into a readable baseline?
- Does the graph draw in the order a human would read it?
- Does the camera decelerate before the viewer needs to parse text?
- Does the transition preserve spatial memory?
- Does the UI feel interactive and alive without becoming noisy?
