# Camera and Staging

## Principle

Camera behavior is a meaning system. It should reveal product hierarchy, spatial relationships, scale, confidence, and continuity. Do not use camera movement to make weak frames feel more exciting.

## Camera Decision Stack

Before specifying a camera move, answer:

1. What does the viewer need to understand that cannot be shown from the current view?
2. Does the move reveal detail, reveal context, compare states, create scale, or transfer attention?
3. What is the focal object before, during, and after the move?
4. Where does the move resolve?
5. Does the viewer have enough time to read the result?

If the answer is "it looks dynamic", keep the camera still and improve the composition.

## Shot Scale

Use shot scale like film language:

- **Macro detail**: Cursor, button, metric, status pill, text input, small state change. Use for proof and tactile trust.
- **Close product crop**: One feature region, chart, panel, table, or command surface. Use for capability proof.
- **Medium product view**: A full product screen or hero panel. Use for product recognition.
- **Wide system view**: Multiple panels, nodes, teams, or integrations. Use for platform breadth.
- **World view**: Abstract brand world, category shift, or system-scale effect. Use for hooks and chapter transitions.

Do not start every sequence wide. Premium product films often earn the wide view by first proving a specific detail.

## Camera Moves

### Push In

Use to increase importance, enter a feature, or make a claim feel focused.

Rules:

- Resolve on a readable detail.
- Decelerate before dense UI or text.
- Avoid pushing into a visually empty center.

### Pull Back

Use to reveal system breadth, consequences, or the relationship between parts.

Rules:

- Start with a meaningful detail.
- Reveal context that changes the viewer's understanding.
- End on a composed system shape, not scattered objects.

### Lateral Track

Use to move through workflow stages, compare states, or imply product momentum.

Rules:

- Keep the reading direction consistent.
- Make each stop a clear idea.
- Avoid side-scrolling through identical cards.

### Vertical Move

Use to express hierarchy, drill-down, elevation, approval, or stack depth.

Rules:

- Downward moves imply detail, diagnosis, or inspection.
- Upward moves imply overview, status, progress, or launch.
- Keep UI planes aligned enough to preserve orientation.

### Orbit

Use rarely. Orbit only when the object has meaningful dimensionality or when the changing angle reveals relationships.

Rules:

- Keep the orbit shallow for SaaS UI.
- Do not orbit flat screens for decoration.
- Stop on the most readable angle.

### Rack Focus or Depth Shift

Use to transfer attention between foreground and background objects.

Rules:

- Blur only what the viewer no longer needs.
- Do not blur text that must be read.
- Pair focus shift with a narrative handoff.

### Locked-Off Hold

Use to make the product feel confident.

Rules:

- Hold after major reveals.
- Hold during dense UI proof.
- Let internal UI motion carry the beat while the camera stays stable.

## Spatial Staging

Stage product objects by meaning:

- Center: hero product, final resolve, core claim.
- Left to right: process, timeline, workflow, input to output.
- Foreground: active feature, current decision, user action.
- Background: context, history, supporting data, inactive system state.
- Above: command, overview, intelligence, governance.
- Below: details, logs, evidence, implementation.

Keep recurring concepts in stable zones across shots. Spatial consistency makes complex products feel easier.

## 2D, 2.5D, and 3D Restraint

Use 2D when legibility is the priority.

Use 2.5D when hierarchy, layering, or product breadth matters.

Use 3D when the product needs a hero object, spatial metaphor, or cinematic reveal.

Avoid 3D when:

- the UI becomes unreadable
- the motion is only ornamental
- perspective distorts product truth
- a simple product action would be more credible

## Lens and Perspective Feel

Even when no real lens is used, specify the lens feeling:

- Orthographic: precise, systematic, design-led, diagrammatic.
- Mild perspective: premium product depth without distortion.
- Close perspective: tactile detail, product materiality, dramatic focus.
- Wide perspective: scale and energy, but risky for readable UI.

Most SaaS product motion should live between orthographic and mild perspective.

## Camera Anti-Patterns

- endless drift with no reveal
- orbiting flat UI panels
- pushing through unreadable text
- zooming only because the shot feels static
- wide views with no focal hierarchy
- camera moves that fight type readability
- perspective so strong the product looks fake
